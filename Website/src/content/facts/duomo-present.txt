DUOMO DI MILANO — PRESENT DAY
Source: Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo (https://www.duomomilano.it); Comune di Milano

SCALE AND STATISTICS
The Duomo di Milano is the largest Gothic cathedral in the world and the third-largest Christian church overall, after St Peter's Basilica in Rome and the Cathedral of Seville. It measures 157 metres in length, 92 metres in width, and covers an area of approximately 11,700 square metres. The interior can hold up to 40,000 people. The cathedral has 135 spires, 3,400 statues on its exterior (more than any other building in the world), and 52 pillars inside the nave.

LA MADONNINA
The most iconic element of the Duomo is La Madonnina — the gilded copper statue of the Virgin Mary that crowns the tallest spire at a height of 108.5 metres. The statue, which stands 4.16 metres tall, was sculpted by Giuseppe Bini and placed in position in 1774. She is covered in gold leaf and is visible from many parts of Milan on a clear day. A beloved Milanese custom holds that no building in the city should exceed her height — an informal rule respected until 1958, when the Pirelli Tower became the first skyscraper to rise above her. The tradition gave rise to the popular Milanese song "O mia bela Madunina."

THE ROOF TERRACES
One of the most unique experiences the Duomo offers is the chance to walk on its roof terraces, up among the spires and statues. Visitors can access the terraces by stairs or lift from the side of the cathedral. From the roof, the forest of Gothic pinnacles, flying buttresses, and marble statues is visible up close, along with panoramic views of Milan and, on clear days, the Alps to the north. The terraces have been open to the public since the Napoleonic era.

THE VENERANDA FABBRICA DEL DUOMO
The Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo — the body founded in 1387 to oversee construction — still exists and operates today, making it one of the oldest continuously operating institutions in the world. Its primary role is now the conservation and restoration of the cathedral. The Fabbrica still quarries Candoglia marble for restoration work using the same perpetual grant issued by Gian Galeazzo Visconti in 1387. A team of sculptors, stonemasons, and restoration specialists works on the building year-round.

THE SOLAR MERIDIAN LINE
Embedded in the floor of the Duomo is a brass solar meridian line, installed in 1786 by the astronomer Giovanni Angelo Cesaris. A small hole in the wall allows a beam of sunlight to hit the line at solar noon, and the position of the beam along the line indicates the time of year. The meridian was used to accurately set clocks and verify the calendar before modern timekeeping instruments. It is one of the largest solar meridians in the world and is still functional today.

THE HOLY NAIL
The Duomo houses one of Christianity's most venerated relics — a nail said to be from the True Cross of Christ. The nail (known as the "Sacro Chiodo") is kept in a reliquary high above the altar, marked by a red light visible from the nave. Once a year, on the Saturday closest to 14 September (the Feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross), the Archbishop of Milan ascends to the reliquary in a ceremony using a special elevator designed by Leonardo da Vinci — an apparatus still used today in its reconstructed form.

VISITOR INFORMATION
The Duomo is one of the most visited attractions in Italy, receiving approximately 5 million visitors per year. The cathedral itself is free to enter for worship, while tickets are required for the roof terraces, the archaeological area beneath the cathedral (which contains the remains of the earlier Baptistery of St John), and the Museo del Duomo (located in Palazzo Reale). The piazza in front of the cathedral is a major public gathering space and the symbolic centre of Milan.
