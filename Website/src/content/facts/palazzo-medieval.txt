PALAZZO REALE — MEDIEVAL / EARLY MODERN PERIOD (12th century – 1500s)
Source: Palazzo Reale Milano (https://www.palazzorealemilano.it); Comune di Milano

ORIGINS AS THE COMMUNAL SEAT
The building that would become Palazzo Reale began as the "Broletto Nuovo" — the seat of Milan's medieval communal government — in the 12th century. Milan at this time was one of the most powerful cities in northern Italy and a leading member of the Lombard League, the alliance of northern Italian communes that successfully resisted the Holy Roman Emperor Frederick Barbarossa. The Broletto Nuovo served as the city's main civic building, housing the administrative offices of the commune, the law courts, and the council chambers. It stood on the south side of Piazza del Duomo, facing the cathedral.

THE VISCONTI TAKEOVER
In the late 13th century, the Visconti family — who would dominate Milan for over a century — seized control of the city and transformed the communal seat into their ducal residence. The building was extensively rebuilt and expanded to reflect the power and ambition of the Visconti dynasty. Under Gian Galeazzo Visconti (the same patron who commissioned the Duomo in 1386), the palace became one of the grandest ducal residences in Italy. The Visconti used the palace as the administrative, judicial, and ceremonial centre of their state.

THE SFORZA DYNASTY
After the fall of the Visconti in 1447, power in Milan passed briefly to the Ambrosian Republic and then to the Sforza dynasty, who ruled until 1499. The Sforza were great patrons of the arts and transformed the palace into a Renaissance cultural centre. Francesco Sforza and his successors hosted artists, architects, engineers, and humanist scholars from across Italy. The palace was expanded, decorated with frescoes, and furnished with works of art that made it one of the leading cultural centres of the Italian Renaissance.

LEONARDO DA VINCI AT COURT
The most famous figure associated with the Sforza court was Leonardo da Vinci, who lived and worked in Milan from 1482 to 1499 under the patronage of Ludovico Sforza ("il Moro"). Leonardo was employed as a painter, sculptor, engineer, and court entertainer. He designed theatrical sets and costumes for court festivals, painted portraits of Sforza courtiers (including the "Lady with an Ermine," believed to depict Ludovico's mistress Cecilia Gallerani), and worked on engineering projects for the city's canal system. He also began work on a colossal bronze equestrian statue of Francesco Sforza — a project that was never completed. During this period Leonardo painted "The Last Supper" in the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie, just a short distance from the palace. The palace itself is believed to have housed Leonardo's studio for part of this period.

THE FRENCH CONQUEST
In 1499 the French king Louis XII invaded Milan and expelled Ludovico Sforza. The Sforza court — including Leonardo — scattered. The palace was occupied by French governors, then briefly returned to Sforza control before being definitively incorporated into the Habsburg empire in 1535. Under the Spanish Habsburgs, the building underwent further changes and served as the residence of the Spanish governors of Milan. The medieval and Renaissance character of the interior was progressively replaced by Baroque decoration.
