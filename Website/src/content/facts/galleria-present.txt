GALLERIA VITTORIO EMANUELE II — PRESENT DAY
Source: Comune di Milano (https://www.comune.milano.it); Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II official documentation

ARCHITECTURE AND DESIGN
The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II was designed by architect Giuseppe Mengoni and constructed between 1865 and 1877. It is one of the oldest and most beautiful shopping arcades in the world. The Galleria has a cross-shaped floor plan, with two glass-vaulted arms intersecting at a central octagonal space (the "ottagono") topped by a large iron-and-glass dome rising 47 metres above the mosaic floor. The architectural style is eclectic, blending Neo-Renaissance facades with the innovative use of iron and glass that was pioneered in mid-19th-century engineering. The exterior triumphal arch facing Piazza del Duomo was designed to create a grand formal entrance to the arcade.

THE TRAGIC DEATH OF MENGONI
Giuseppe Mengoni, the architect who devoted over a decade of his life to the Galleria, never lived to see the official opening ceremony. On 30 December 1877 — just two days before the inauguration — Mengoni fell from the scaffolding near the top of the central dome and died. The cause of the fall has never been definitively established. The Galleria was inaugurated on 1 January 1878 in a ceremony attended by King Umberto I, but without its creator.

THE MOSAIC FLOOR AND THE BULL OF TURIN
The floor of the Galleria is decorated with elaborate mosaics representing the coats of arms of the four capital cities of the Italian peninsula at the time of unification: Milan (a white cross on a red background), Turin (a bull on a red background), Florence (a lily on a white background), and Rome (an eagle on a red background). The bull of Turin, located at the centre of the octagonal crossing, has become the focus of a popular tradition: visitors spin on the heel of their right foot on the bull's testicles for good luck. The mosaic is visibly worn from centuries of this practice. The tradition is so well known that it attracts visitors specifically to perform it.

HISTORIC SHOPS AND RESTAURANTS
The Galleria houses some of Milan's most historic and prestigious establishments. The Savini restaurant has operated in the Galleria since 1867, making it one of the oldest restaurants in Milan. The fashion house Prada opened its first store in the Galleria in 1913, and the original Prada shop — now a flagship store — remains in its original location. Other historic establishments include the Biffi Caffè and the Campari bar, which opened in 1867. The luxury brands and boutiques in the Galleria today include Louis Vuitton, Gucci, Ferrari, and Tod's.

CONNECTION BETWEEN TWO PIAZZAS
The Galleria serves a practical urban function as a covered pedestrian passage connecting Piazza del Duomo to Piazza della Scala — home of the Teatro alla Scala, one of the world's most famous opera houses. This connection made the Galleria a natural social hub, as it sits between Milan's two most important public spaces. The evening passeggiata through the Galleria, followed by a coffee at one of the historic bars, remains a quintessential Milanese experience.

THE GALLERIA AS CIVIC SYMBOL
The Galleria was named after Vittorio Emanuele II, the first king of unified Italy, to celebrate the unification of the Italian peninsula in 1861. It was conceived not just as a commercial space but as a monument to the new nation — a grand civic building that expressed confidence in Italy's future. Today it remains one of the most photographed and visited landmarks in Milan, with millions of tourists passing through it every year alongside local Milanese who use it as an everyday shortcut and gathering place.
