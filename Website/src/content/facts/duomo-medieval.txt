DUOMO DI MILANO — MEDIEVAL PERIOD (1386 – ~1500)
Source: Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo (https://www.duomomilano.it)

FOUNDATION AND PATRON
Construction of the Duomo di Milano began on 15 March 1386 under the patronage of Gian Galeazzo Visconti, the Duke of Milan, and Archbishop Antonio da Saluzzo. Visconti saw the cathedral as a statement of dynastic power and civic pride. He personally funded much of the early construction and granted the Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo — the building authority created specifically for the project — perpetual rights to the Candoglia marble quarry in 1387, a grant that remains in force to this day.

ARCHITECTURAL STYLE
The Duomo was designed in the Flamboyant Gothic style, which was unusual for Italy at the time. Most Italian churches of the period followed Romanesque or early Renaissance conventions. Visconti deliberately brought in French and German master builders — including Nicolas de Bonaventure and Heinrich Parler — to achieve the northern European Gothic look. The design features pointed arches, flying buttresses, elaborate pinnacles, and a forest of spires rising from the roofline, all characteristics of northern Gothic architecture.

MATERIALS: THE PINK MARBLE OF CANDOGLIA
Almost the entire cathedral is built from Candoglia marble, a distinctive pink-grey stone quarried in the Ossola Valley, approximately 100 kilometres north of Milan. The marble was transported down the River Toce and then along canals (the Navigli) directly to the construction site. Visconti's 1387 charter granted the Fabbrica the right to extract marble from Candoglia free of tax, under the Latin inscription "AUF" (Ad Usum Fabricae — for the use of the building works). This tax-exempt status applied only to the Duomo; any marble diverted elsewhere was confiscated. The quarry is still operated by the Fabbrica today for restoration work.

SCALE AND AMBITION
The Duomo was planned as one of the largest Christian churches in the world. The original design called for a five-nave basilica with a capacity of 40,000 people, 135 spires, and over 3,400 statues. The sheer scale of the project meant that construction would span centuries. Medieval builders estimated the project would take generations, and they were correct — the cathedral was not formally completed until 1965, nearly six centuries after the first stone was laid.

STRUCTURAL INNOVATION
The medieval builders faced significant engineering challenges. The soft Lombard soil required deep foundations. The weight of the marble structure demanded a complex system of internal buttresses and iron tie-rods to hold the walls in tension. The builders also experimented with the proportions of the nave, debating between the "ad quadratum" (square-based) and "ad triangulum" (triangle-based) systems — a debate that attracted mathematicians and engineers from across Europe and is documented in the Fabbrica's archives.

CULTURAL SIGNIFICANCE
The Duomo was not just a place of worship — it was the heart of Milanese civic identity. The Visconti and later the Sforza dynasty used ceremonies at the Duomo to legitimise their rule. Coronations, funerals, and public celebrations all took place in and around the cathedral. The construction site itself was a major employer, supporting hundreds of skilled craftsmen, sculptors, marble cutters, and labourers for generations.
