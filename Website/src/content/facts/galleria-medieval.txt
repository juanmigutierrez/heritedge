GALLERIA VITTORIO EMANUELE II — MEDIEVAL / PRE-GALLERIA PERIOD (before 1865)
Source: Archivio Storico Civico di Milano; Comune di Milano

THE REBECCHINO NEIGHBOURHOOD
Before the Galleria was built, the area between Piazza del Duomo and Piazza della Scala was occupied by a dense medieval neighbourhood known as the "Rebecchino." This was a tightly packed urban quarter typical of medieval Milan — narrow cobbled streets, low stone buildings, small churches, artisan workshops, and residential courtyards. The neighbourhood had evolved organically over centuries, with street patterns following Roman-era land divisions. It was a working-class and commercial district, home to merchants, craftsmen, and small traders who served the cathedral and the city centre.

STREET PATTERN AND URBAN FABRIC
The medieval street network in this area was a labyrinth of vicoli (alleyways) and small piazzas. Several of these streets had been in continuous use since the Roman period, when Mediolanum (ancient Milan) had a regular grid layout. Over the medieval centuries, the grid had been partially obscured by informal construction, market stalls, and encroachments. The area also contained several small churches and oratories, including the church of San Giovanni in Conca (remnants of which survive underground today).

SOCIAL AND COMMERCIAL LIFE
The Rebecchino was a lively commercial hub. Markets were held in the streets near the Duomo, selling food, cloth, leather goods, and metalwork. The proximity to the cathedral made the area commercially valuable, as pilgrims, clergy, and city officials passed through daily. Many of the buildings were a mix of ground-floor shops and upper-floor residences — a typology (called "casa-bottega") common in medieval Italian cities.

DEMOLITION FOR THE GALLERIA
In the 1860s, following Italian unification, the new government of Milan undertook a major urban renewal project to modernise the city centre. The architect Giuseppe Mengoni was commissioned to design a grand covered arcade connecting Piazza del Duomo to Piazza della Scala. The project required the complete demolition of the Rebecchino neighbourhood. Hundreds of families and businesses were displaced. The demolition was controversial at the time, as many Milanese were attached to the medieval urban fabric, but the project was pushed through as a symbol of modern, unified Italy.

ARCHAEOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE
Excavations carried out before and during the construction of the Galleria uncovered significant archaeological remains from the Roman and medieval periods, including mosaic floors, architectural fragments, and everyday objects. Some of these finds are preserved in the Civico Museo Archeologico di Milano. The construction of the Galleria effectively buried and preserved a large section of medieval Milan beneath its foundations.
