PALAZZO REALE — POST-WAR PERIOD (1943 – 1970s)
Source: Palazzo Reale Milano (https://www.palazzorealemilano.it/storia); Archivio Storico Civico di Milano

THE 1943 BOMBING
Palazzo Reale was one of the most significant buildings damaged in the Allied bombing raids of August 1943. Incendiary bombs struck the palace and caused devastating fires that burned for hours. The fire destroyed or severely damaged much of the palace's interior, including irreplaceable 18th-century Neoclassical decorations, frescoes, furnishings, and artworks that had accumulated over centuries. The palace's roof was partly destroyed and several rooms were left open to the sky.

THE SALA DELLE CARIATIDI
The most dramatic casualty of the bombing was the Sala delle Cariatidi — the grand ballroom on the piano nobile (principal floor) of the palace, named for the carved female figures (caryatids) that supported the elaborate ceiling. The room had been one of the most celebrated Neoclassical interiors in Milan, designed in the 18th century and used for royal receptions and state ceremonies. The bombing and subsequent fire destroyed the ceiling almost entirely and severely damaged the walls and the caryatid figures themselves. The room was left as a charred, partially open ruin after the war.

PICASSO'S GUERNICA IN THE BOMBED ROOM
In a deeply symbolic decision, the organisers of a major Picasso retrospective in 1953 chose to exhibit the painter's monumental anti-war work "Guernica" in the Sala delle Cariatidi — displaying a painting about the horror of aerial bombing in a room that had itself been destroyed by aerial bombing. The juxtaposition was internationally noted and made a powerful statement about the violence of modern warfare. The exhibition drew enormous crowds and was one of the most significant cultural events in post-war Milan.

THE DECISION NOT TO RESTORE
After the war, a deliberate decision was made not to fully restore the Sala delle Cariatidi. The partially destroyed room — with its blackened walls, missing ceiling, and damaged statues — was preserved as a permanent memorial to the destruction of the war. This was an unusual choice for the time, when most war-damaged buildings were restored to their pre-war appearance. The decision reflected a belief that the wounds of war should remain visible as a reminder and a warning. The Sala delle Cariatidi remains partially unrestored today, and is one of the most moving and unusual spaces in any public building in Milan.

POST-WAR RECONSTRUCTION AND REOPENING
The rest of Palazzo Reale underwent gradual restoration throughout the late 1940s and 1950s. The work was complex and expensive, as many of the original craftsmen who had maintained the Neoclassical interiors were no longer alive and many of the original materials were unavailable. The palace reopened progressively — room by room — as a public cultural venue rather than as a private royal or governmental building. The decision to open the palace to the public as an exhibition space was part of the broader democratisation of Italian cultural institutions in the post-war period.

MILAN'S CULTURAL RENAISSANCE
The restoration of Palazzo Reale coincided with Milan's emergence as the cultural capital of Italy in the 1950s and 1960s. The palace hosted major exhibitions of modern and historical art, attracting international visitors and establishing Milan as a world-class cultural destination. The combination of the historic Neoclassical setting and the contemporary art exhibitions created a distinctive identity for Palazzo Reale that it maintains to this day.
