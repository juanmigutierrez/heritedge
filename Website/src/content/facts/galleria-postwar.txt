GALLERIA VITTORIO EMANUELE II — POST-WAR PERIOD (1943 – 1960s)
Source: Archivio Storico Civico di Milano; Comune di Milano

THE 1943 BOMBING
The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II was severely damaged in the Allied bombing raids of August 1943. Incendiary bombs destroyed large sections of the iron-and-glass roof that covered the arcade's two main arms and the central octagonal dome. The fire that followed the bombing gutted portions of the interior, destroying decorative elements, shop interiors, and the elaborate mosaic floors in some sections. Photographs from the period show the Galleria open to the sky, its elegant iron framework blackened by fire and its glass panels shattered. Several of the historic shops and restaurants that had operated in the Galleria for decades were destroyed.

WARTIME SIGNIFICANCE
Despite the damage, the Galleria retained its role as a gathering place for Milanese during the war years. The covered arcade — even partially open to the sky — offered some shelter and remained a point of congregation for citizens navigating the bombed city. The destruction of the glass roof was widely seen as a symbolic blow to Milan's civic pride, as the Galleria had been one of the most celebrated examples of 19th-century Italian architecture and engineering.

RECONSTRUCTION
Reconstruction of the Galleria began almost immediately after the end of the war in 1945. The iron framework of the roof had survived largely intact, and the task was primarily to replace the thousands of glass panels that had been destroyed. The reconstruction was completed by 1948, and the Galleria reopened to the public as one of the first major civic buildings in Milan to be fully restored. The speed of the reconstruction — just three years — was seen as a statement of Milanese determination and was celebrated in the press as a symbol of the city's recovery.

THE "MIRACOLO ECONOMICO" AND THE GALLERIA
The late 1950s and 1960s saw Milan become the economic capital of Italy during the country's post-war economic miracle. The Galleria, fully restored, became once again the social and commercial heart of the city. Its historic cafes and restaurants — including the Savini, which had first opened in 1867 — were patronised by the new Milanese business class, architects, designers, and intellectuals who were transforming Milan into a global fashion and design capital. The Galleria was the place to be seen, to close deals, and to celebrate successes.

CULTURAL LIFE
The post-war Galleria became an important hub for Milanese cultural life. Publishers, journalists, and intellectuals gathered in its cafes. The proximity to La Scala opera house (just steps away across Piazza della Scala) made the Galleria a natural gathering place before and after performances. The tradition of the "passeggiata" — the evening stroll through the Galleria — became firmly established during this period as a ritual of Milanese social life.
