DUOMO DI MILANO — POST-WAR PERIOD (1943 – 1960s)
Source: Archivio Storico Civico di Milano; Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo

THE 1943 BOMBING
On the night of 12–13 August 1943, Allied air forces carried out a heavy bombing raid on Milan as part of the campaign to force Italy's surrender. The raid targeted the industrial and railway infrastructure of the city but caused widespread civilian damage. Several bombs struck the area around Piazza del Duomo. The cathedral itself was hit — incendiary bombs damaged the roof of the nave and destroyed or damaged stained-glass windows, interior furnishings, and structural elements on the north side. However, the main Gothic structure — the walls, spires, and marble cladding — survived largely intact, partly due to the extraordinary thickness of the marble construction.

WARTIME USE OF THE CATHEDRAL
During the war years, the Duomo served multiple roles beyond worship. Parts of the cathedral and the surrounding buildings were used to shelter displaced civilians whose homes had been destroyed in the bombing raids. The piazza became a gathering point for Milanese during air raid alerts, and the cathedral's bell tower was used as an observation post. Church services continued throughout the war, and the Duomo became a symbol of resilience for the city's population.

THE LIBERATION OF MILAN
On 25 April 1945 — now celebrated as Liberation Day (Festa della Liberazione) across Italy — Milan was liberated from Nazi-Fascist occupation by partisan forces. Piazza del Duomo was the symbolic centre of the liberation. Enormous crowds gathered in the square to celebrate, and the event was photographed and filmed extensively. The date 25 April remains one of the most emotionally significant in Milanese collective memory, and the piazza continues to be the gathering point for Liberation Day commemorations every year.

POST-WAR RECONSTRUCTION
The immediate post-war years saw urgent restoration work on the cathedral. Damaged windows were replaced or temporarily boarded up. Structural surveys were carried out to assess the impact of the bombing. The Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo — which had continued to operate throughout the war — coordinated the restoration effort. The work was complicated by the severe economic conditions of post-war Italy, and full restoration took many years. The reconstruction of the Duomo's damaged elements was seen as part of the broader civic effort to rebuild Milan's identity after the destruction of the war.

COMPLETING THE FACADE
One of the most significant milestones of the post-war decades was the formal completion of the Duomo's facade. Although construction had begun in 1386, the main facade had never been fully finished. The last bronze doors — the fifth and final set — were installed in 1965, marking the official completion of the cathedral, 579 years after construction began. The completion was celebrated as a major cultural event for Milan and for Italy.

MILAN'S ECONOMIC RECOVERY
The post-war period coincided with Italy's "miracolo economico" (economic miracle) of the late 1950s and 1960s, during which Milan became the industrial and financial capital of Italy. The restoration of the Duomo and the rebuilding of Piazza del Duomo were symbolic of this broader recovery. The piazza was also redesigned and pedestrianised during this period, transforming it into the open civic space it is today.
