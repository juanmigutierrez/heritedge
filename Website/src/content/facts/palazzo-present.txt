PALAZZO REALE — PRESENT DAY
Source: Palazzo Reale Milano (https://www.palazzorealemilano.it); Comune di Milano

THE BUILDING TODAY
Palazzo Reale occupies the entire south side of Piazza del Duomo, directly opposite the cathedral. The Neoclassical facade — designed by the architect Giuseppe Piermarini in the late 18th century for the Austrian Empress Maria Theresa — presents a long, austere front of grey stone to the piazza, punctuated by tall windows and a central entrance portal. The building is owned by the Comune di Milano (City of Milan) and is managed as a public cultural institution. Entry to the courtyard is free; entry to the exhibition spaces requires a ticket.

MAJOR EXHIBITION VENUE
Today Palazzo Reale is one of the most important temporary exhibition venues in Italy, hosting between five and eight major exhibitions per year covering art from antiquity to the present day. Past exhibitions have featured works by Monet, Van Gogh, Picasso, Caravaggio, Raphael, and many other major figures in art history, as well as contemporary artists and designers. The palace attracts over 600,000 visitors annually, making it one of the most visited cultural venues in Milan. Its central location on Piazza del Duomo means it benefits from the enormous flow of tourists visiting the cathedral.

THE SALA DELLE CARIATIDI TODAY
The Sala delle Cariatidi remains one of the most distinctive and emotionally powerful spaces in the building. Deliberately preserved in its partially destroyed post-war state, the room is used for large-scale art installations and special exhibitions. The blackened walls, the missing ceiling sections, and the damaged caryatid figures create a unique atmosphere unlike any other exhibition space in the world. The room is regularly cited by visitors as one of the most memorable spaces they have encountered. It serves simultaneously as an art venue and as a permanent memorial to the 1943 bombing.

THE MUSEO DEL DUOMO
Part of Palazzo Reale houses the Museo del Duomo, which contains an extensive collection of artefacts, sculptures, stained glass, tapestries, and architectural models related to the history and construction of the cathedral across the piazza. The museum includes original Gothic sculptures removed from the exterior of the Duomo during restoration work, medieval and Renaissance religious artefacts, and a large-scale model of the cathedral showing its construction at various stages. The Museo del Duomo provides essential context for understanding the Duomo and is considered an important complement to a visit to the cathedral itself.

NEOCLASSICAL INTERIORS
The rooms that were successfully restored after the war retain their 18th-century Neoclassical character — high ceilings with elaborate plasterwork, painted friezes, parquet floors, and tall windows overlooking the piazza. These rooms are used both for permanent displays related to the palace's history and as the backdrop for temporary exhibitions. The contrast between the pristine Neoclassical rooms and the deliberately unrestored Sala delle Cariatidi creates a powerful dialogue about history, destruction, and memory within the same building.

HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE
Palazzo Reale has been at the centre of Milanese and Italian history for nearly a thousand years. It has served as the seat of communal government, the residence of the Visconti and Sforza dukes, the court of Leonardo da Vinci, the palace of Spanish and Austrian governors, the royal residence of the Savoy kings, and finally a democratic public museum. No other building in Milan contains such a layered and continuous history, and few buildings in Italy have witnessed as many turning points in the history of the nation.
