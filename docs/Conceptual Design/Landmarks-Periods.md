# Piazza del Duomo — Three Lives of Three Buildings

A research dossier for HeritEdge content. Each landmark is told through the same three life-moments — **Origins**, **Transformation**, **Today** — alongside the **daily life** of Milanese people in that period, and a pair of side-by-side panels noting practices that have *died out* and rituals that *emerged later*.

Compiled for the Polimi MITA project · Twilight Piazza chapter content.

## 1. Duomo di Milano

*Italian Gothic on a Lombard scale. A six-century building site that still hasn't really closed.*

### Origins · 1386 — 1500

The first stone was laid on **5 August 1386**, under Archbishop Antonio da Saluzzo. Milan was an ambitious city under the **Visconti** dynasty, and Duke Gian Galeazzo Visconti adopted the cathedral as a state project — a way to put Milan on the European stage alongside Cologne, Reims and Bourges. The Gothic style was an unusual choice for Lombardy, which had built in Romanesque for centuries; choosing Gothic was a political signal.

A dedicated institution, the **Veneranda Fabbrica del Duomo** ("Venerable Factory of the Duomo"), was founded in 1387 to oversee the works. It is one of the longest-running construction organisations in human history — *it still operates today*. The first chief architect was **Simone da Orsenigo**. The cathedral's marble was — and still is — quarried from **Candoglia** in Piedmont, a site that Gian Galeazzo Visconti gave to the Fabbrica in perpetuity. [^1] [^2]

> **Story · AUF on the marble trucks.** To this day, lorries carrying Candoglia marble for the Duomo are painted with the letters "AUF" — short for *Ad Usum Fabricae*, "for the use of the Fabbrica". Historically these shipments were exempt from tolls under the Visconti grant, and the abbreviation passed into Milanese slang: to do something *a ufo* means to do it for free. [^1]

> **Story · "Lungo come la fabbrica del Duomo."** A Lombard idiom — *"as long as the construction of the Duomo"* — used for any endlessly drawn-out task. [^3]

#### Life in Milan, 1386 — 1500

Milan in 1386 was a walled city of roughly **80,000 to 100,000 inhabitants** — large by medieval European standards. [^4] The economy was organised around the **arti** (guilds): wool-workers, silk-weavers, goldsmiths, dyers, tanners, bakers, armourers. Each guild had its own patron saint, processional banner, and seat near Piazza dei Mercanti, which functioned as the daily commercial and civic centre — the Piazza del Duomo as we know it did not yet exist; the cathedral was rising at the southern edge of a dense knot of canons' houses and small churches that would be cleared away only centuries later. [^5]

The Visconti court patronised musicians, illuminators, and humanist scholars; the duke kept a *cancelleria* of secretaries who wrote in elegant Latin. But for most Milanese, life was punctuated by **bells**: matins woke the city before dawn, the *ora di marcato* opened the markets, and the cathedral's evening bell — the *campanone* — sent labourers home. The construction of the Duomo itself was an enormous engine of employment: stonemasons, sculptors, carpenters, glassmakers and unskilled labourers worked the site, and the Fabbrica's payrolls (still preserved) are among the most detailed labour records of medieval Italy. [^1] [^6]

**Plague** was constant company. The **Black Death of 1348** had killed perhaps one in three Milanese a generation before the Duomo began, and outbreaks continued. The cult of plague-saints — Sebastian, Roch — was bound up with cathedral devotion. [^7]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Sumptuary laws** dictated who could wear what. Furs, silk, and the colour scarlet were reserved for nobility; merchants' wives faced fines for ornament considered above their station. [^7]
> - **Public penance** for sins like blasphemy or fraud — barefoot, in a sack, holding a candle outside the Duomo's growing walls.
> - **Joust tournaments** were held in the open space south of the cathedral on major Visconti feast-days, with knights in full plate.
> - **Hired mourners** (*prefiche*) walked behind funeral processions for a fee, weeping and singing dirges.
> - **Bell-paced labour** — work began and ended by the cathedral's bells, not a clock. The first public clock in Milan, on the Palazzo della Ragione, was a curiosity, not a regulator of daily time.

> **New rituals · what we do now that they didn't**
>
> - Drinking espresso standing at a bar (coffee did not reach Italy until the late 1500s). [^8]
> - **Aperitivo** before dinner (an invention of the 1800s — see Galleria, below).
> - Visiting the Duomo as a *tourist* rather than a parishioner.
> - Walking the rooftop terraces (opened to the public in 1956).

### Transformation · 1500 — 1813

The **tiburio** — the central crossing tower over the dome — was the great structural problem of the late 1400s. **Leonardo da Vinci** submitted a wooden model and drawings in 1487–88; his sketches survive in the *Codex Atlanticus*. Bramante also proposed designs. The Lombard architects Giovanni Antonio Amadeo and Giovanni Giacomo Dolcebuono ultimately won the commission; the tiburio was structurally complete by 1500. [^9]

The 1500s and 1600s brought the heavy hand of **San Carlo Borromeo** (1538–1584), Archbishop and saint, who imposed Counter-Reformation austerity, removing many medieval features. The interior we see today is largely his. [^10]

In 1762 the Fabbrica approved the design for a **gilded copper statue of the Virgin Mary** to crown the central spire — sculptor Giuseppe Perego, goldsmith Giuseppe Bini. The statue — affectionately *la Madonnina* — was placed at 108.5 m above ground on **30 December 1774**. [^1]

> **Story · The unspoken Milanese pact.** Tradition holds that no Milanese building should rise above the Madonnina. When the Pirelli Tower (127 m, 1960) and later the UniCredit Tower (231 m, 2011) broke the rule, the Milanese demanded — and got — small replicas of her placed on each rooftop. She is technically on the highest point of the city in three places at once. [^11]

In 1805 **Napoleon Bonaparte** crowned himself King of Italy inside the Duomo, placing the *Iron Crown of Lombardy* on his own head. He funded the long-stalled completion of the **façade**, which was finished — at least in its main lines — by 1813. His name is carved on it. [^12]

#### Life in Milan, 1500 — 1813

This is the era of Milan as a **Spanish, then Austrian, possession** — the city was no longer a sovereign duchy but a province of an empire whose centre lay over the Alps. The Habsburgs kept Milan productive but politically quiet. Population stagnated through the 1600s — the **plague of 1576** (the *peste di San Carlo*) killed an estimated 17,000 Milanese; the more famous **1630 plague**, immortalised by Manzoni in *I Promessi Sposi*, killed perhaps half the surviving population. [^4] [^13]

What did people do, day to day? Aristocrats moved between their *palazzi* in the centre and country villas in the Brianza, attended Mass at the Duomo (often through the family's private chapel), played cards, took the carriage promenade along the Bastioni (the city walls). The middle ranks — notaries, doctors, larger merchants — gathered in the early **caffè** that began appearing in the late 1600s; by the 1760s Milan had several, including the Caffè Demetrio that gave its name to the Enlightenment journal *Il Caffè* edited by Pietro and Alessandro Verri. [^14]

Saffron-yellow **risotto alla milanese** appears in cookbooks of the 1700s, but the legend traces it earlier: a Belgian glass-painter known as *Maestro Valerio di Fiandra*, working on the Duomo's stained glass, supposedly used powdered saffron to colour his glass and — at the 1574 wedding of his daughter — instructed the cook to add saffron to the rice. Whether or not the tale is true, it is the most repeated "origin story" in Milanese food culture. [^15]

In **1778**, the **Teatro alla Scala** opened, and within a generation opera-going had become the central social activity of the Milanese upper classes. **Private theatre boxes were owned like apartments** — families inherited them, decorated their interiors, and entertained guests during performances; the music was sometimes incidental to the conversation. [^16]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Carrying personal swords** — gentlemen wore a small sword as part of daily dress through most of the 1600s.
> - **Sedan chairs** (*portantine*) carried by hired bearers were Milan's high-end transport before carriages.
> - **Private opera boxes** as social headquarters — owned, decorated, and used for receiving visitors during performances, not for listening.
> - **Public lotteries** held under religious sponsorship on saints' feast-days.
> - **Domestic Mass** — wealthy families heard daily Mass at home in private chapels, not the parish church.

> **New rituals · what we do now that they didn't**
>
> - Reading the morning newspaper in a café (papers existed but were read at home or aloud at a circle).
> - **Wearing trousers** for women — unthinkable until the 20th century. [^17]
> - Buying *anything* with a card or a phone.
> - Eating dinner at 8 or 9 pm (in the 1700s, the main meal was at midday).

### Today · 1813 — present

The Duomo was declared "complete" in **1965**, when the last great bronze door (designed by Luciano Minguzzi) was installed. But scaffolding remains permanently visible somewhere on the cathedral, and the Fabbrica still employs marble cutters, restorers, and sculptors as permanent staff. Candoglia marble continues to travel down the **Naviglio Grande** canal — the same waterway Visconti used in 1387. [^1] [^2]

The **rooftop terraces** opened to the public via lift in 1956. Walking among the 135 pinnacles is one of the city's defining experiences.

> **Story · WWII narrowly spared her.** During the August 1943 Allied air raids that devastated central Milan, several incendiary bombs hit the cathedral but caused mostly cosmetic damage. The stained-glass windows had been **dismantled and stored** before the raids; they were reinstalled after the war. Nearby Palazzo Reale was not so lucky. [^18]

#### Life in Milan, 1813 — present

This is the century of **Milan-as-engine**. After Italian Unification (1861), the city became the industrial capital of the new kingdom — textiles, then engineering, then publishing, then finance, then design and fashion, in that rough order. Population leapt from around **150,000 in 1860** to over **1 million by 1930** and roughly **1.4 million today**, with about **3.2 million in the metropolitan area**. [^4]

The defining mid-century event was the **economic miracle** (*miracolo economico*) of 1958–1963, when Milan and its hinterland became one of the fastest-growing industrial zones in Europe. Migration from the south transformed neighbourhoods. The post-war decades also gave rise to the cultural exports that now define the city globally: **Milan Fashion Week** (formalised 1958), the **Salone del Mobile** furniture fair (1961), and the rise of *milanese-design* as a brand. [^19]

The Piazza del Duomo itself has been the stage for almost every political moment of the city: **1898**, Bava-Beccaris's troops fired on protesting workers here; **1922 and after**, Mussolini's rallies; **1969**, the Piazza Fontana bombing just two streets away; **2001 onward**, mass demonstrations of every political stripe.

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Sunday closing** of all shops and most cafés (regulated until 2012, when the Monti government partially liberalised retail hours). [^20]
> - **Dressing up for Mass** — even non-believers would put on a suit to cross the Duomo's threshold until the 1970s.
> - **"Vasto"** — informal gathering points where unemployed men waited to be hired by the day for construction or unloading work; common in the post-war decades, almost vanished today.
> - **Smoking indoors** — bars, restaurants, even Milan's metro were smoky until the 2005 national ban.
> - **Lunchtime closure** of the city — most offices and shops closed from roughly 12:30 to 3:30 pm, the *pausa pranzo*, until at least the 1990s. Now mostly continuous-hour ("orario continuato") in the centre.

> **New rituals · what we do now that they didn't**
>
> - **Aperitivo** as a daily institution, often replacing dinner.
> - **Salone del Mobile** week, when the entire city becomes a design fair.
> - **Tourism** as the dominant economic activity in the square itself — locals describe avoiding the Duomo on weekends.
> - **Skateboarding** in front of the cathedral (officially discouraged, practically constant).
> - **Public Wi-Fi**, **contactless payment** for everything including church candles, and **Google-Maps navigation** as the default way to enter the piazza.
> - **Pride parades** ending in Piazza del Duomo (since 1994) — unthinkable for most of Milan's earlier history.

## 2. Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II

*Italy's living drawing room. Iron, glass, and a national mood, all built at once.*

### Origins · 1859 — 1865

The Galleria's site was for centuries a dense knot of **medieval streets** — narrow, dark, lined with workshops, taverns, and the small church of *Santa Tecla*. After **Italian unification in 1861**, the new kingdom wanted a grand civic interior in Milan, its industrial capital. A national competition was launched in 1860, won by **Giuseppe Mengoni** (1829–1877) with a cross-shaped iron-and-glass arcade fusing Renaissance palazzo façades with the engineering of London's Crystal Palace. [^21]

The first stone was laid on **7 March 1865** by King **Vittorio Emanuele II** in person, who would lend his name to the arcade.

> **Story · Built by an English firm.** The iron skeleton of the dome and arms was prefabricated and assembled by **Henry Grissell's** Regent's Canal Ironworks in London. The Galleria is, almost literally, an Italian Renaissance suit worn over a British industrial frame. [^21]

#### Life in Milan, 1859 — 1865

These were the years of the **Risorgimento's last act**. Milan had been ruled by Austria since 1815; the **Five Days of Milan** (18–22 March 1848) — when citizens drove the Austrian garrison out behind barricades made of furniture, mattresses and broken paving stones — had become national legend, even though the Austrians returned within months. The decisive expulsion came in 1859 with French help at the battles of Magenta and Solferino. [^12] [^22]

In that brief window between Austrian and Italian rule, Milan reorganised itself frantically. Streets were renamed (*Corso Francesco* became *Corso Vittorio Emanuele*), the old city walls were marked for demolition, and the new administration laid out boulevards. The Milanese, for the first time in centuries, were citizens of an Italian state rather than subjects of an empire. The Galleria, conceived in 1860–61, was the most public, most architectural expression of that change. [^22]

Daily life still followed older patterns: the workshop-and-home arrangement of artisans, the **balconata** (women leaning out of windows) as a form of street-level communication, **water carriers** (*acquaioli*) supplying houses from public fountains, and **lamplighters** lighting gas lamps each evening. [^23]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Lamplighters** walking the streets at dusk with a long pole.
> - **Water carriers** delivering jugs of fresh water to apartments (Milan only completed its modern aqueduct in 1888).
> - **Gas-lit shopfronts** — the new Galleria's gas chandeliers were a public sensation; people came simply to look at them lit.
> - **Calling cards** left at houses as a formal greeting.

### Transformation · 1865 — 1878

The Galleria rose with extraordinary speed. The glass roof was complete by 1867. The floor mosaics — produced in Venice — were laid in 1875–76; the four medallions at the centre of the octagonal crossing carry the **coats of arms of Rome (she-wolf), Florence (lily), Turin (bull), and Milan (red cross on white)**. [^21]

> **Story · The bull's bad luck.** Tradition demands that visitors plant a heel on the Turin bull's testicles and spin three times for good luck. The tile has been re-cut and replaced multiple times — most recently in 2015. [^24]

> **Story · The architect never saw it open.** On **30 December 1877**, two days before the planned inauguration, **Giuseppe Mengoni fell from the scaffolding** of the triumphal arch and died on the spot. Whether accident, heart attack at altitude, or suicide under financial pressure has never been resolved. [^21]

The **Camparino bar** (the original Bar Campari, founded 1867 by Gaspare Campari) installed itself at the Duomo end; its bitter red aperitif soon became identified with the city itself. [^25]

#### Life in Milan, 1865 — 1878

The Galleria's first decade coincides with what Milanese historians call the **città borghese** moment — a confident, expanding **bourgeoisie** that had replaced both the old aristocracy and the displaced Austrians as the dominant class. They wanted a public stage to display new wealth: top-hatted men, women in crinolines (then bustles), reading the *Corriere della Sera* (founded 1876) at a marble café table. [^26]

The **aperitivo** ritual is born here, with Campari served before dinner in the Galleria's cafés. The word *aperitivo* (from Latin *aperire*, "to open") refers to opening the stomach for the meal to come. **Vermouth** — invented in Turin in the late 1700s — and Campari (1860) were the two foundational drinks; the *Negroni*, *spritz* and *Americano* are all later inventions built on this base. [^25] [^27]

The Galleria also functioned as a **stock exchange of social signalling**. Where you stood, whom you greeted, whose company you kept in the arcade was watched and noted. The 1870s and 80s were the great age of the *flâneur* in Milan — figures like the journalist Carlo Dossi or the editor Eugenio Torelli Viollier (founder of the *Corriere*) crossed the Galleria daily, conducting half their professional life on its mosaic floor. [^26]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Top hats and tail coats** for daytime promenading.
> - **Calling on a woman's day** — every well-off Milanese woman had a designated weekday for receiving visitors at home; the rest of the week was for visiting others.
> - **Reading newspapers aloud in cafés** for the benefit of those who couldn't read.
> - **The morning carriage promenade** along the boulevards — to see and be seen.

> **New rituals · what we do now that they didn't**
>
> - The Spritz (popularised much later, originally Venetian, now everywhere in Milan).
> - Aperitivo *as dinner replacement* — buffet tables of food included in the drink price. This is a late-1990s Milanese innovation, sometimes attributed specifically to bars in the Brera district.

### Today · 1878 — present

The Galleria survived **WWII** with significant damage to the glass roof from the August 1943 bombings; it was rebuilt to the original specifications in the late 1940s. A major façade restoration in **2014–2015** cleaned 150 years of soot from the marble. [^18] [^28]

Tenants today are almost entirely **luxury brands** — Prada (whose first store, founded 1913, is here in its original location), Louis Vuitton, Versace, Gucci. Camparino still operates in its original Liberty-style fittings, and the **Aperitivo** tradition remains a Milanese ritual born in this very arcade. [^25]

#### Life in Milan, 1878 — present

This is the era of Milan as **fashion and design capital**, the home of the *milanese* archetype — busy, business-formal, irreverent under a polished surface. The post-war fashion industry crystallised in the 1950s around couturiers like **Biki**, **Mila Schön**, then in the 1970s with the rise of **Giorgio Armani**, **Gianni Versace**, **Gianfranco Ferré**, and **Miuccia Prada** (whose grandfather's leather-goods store sits in the Galleria itself). The first **Milan Fashion Week** in its modern form was held in 1958; the **Salone del Mobile** furniture fair started in 1961. [^19] [^29]

The most distinctly Milanese contemporary social ritual is the **aperitivo**, now spread to the rest of Italy as a national pastime. The Galleria's cafés — Camparino, Biffi, Savini — are still part of this circuit, though the centre of gravity has shifted to the Brera and Navigli districts, where the buffet-style aperitivo dinner emerged in the 1990s. [^25] [^30]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Smoking inside** Camparino and other Galleria cafés (banned 2005).
> - **Window shopping at night** when the gas lamps were the only public lighting on the street.
> - **Women not entering certain cafés** alone — informal but real until well into the 1960s.

> **New rituals · what we do now that they didn't**
>
> - **Photographing oneself on the bull's mosaic** (the Instagram-era successor to the spinning tradition).
> - **Salone del Mobile week** flooding the Galleria with international designers.
> - **Apple Store visits** as a tourist activity (the Galleria's Apple store opened in 2018).
> - **Wearing trainers** with everything — once unthinkable in central Milan, now the dominant footwear even in luxury settings.

## 3. Palazzo Reale

*A medieval seat of power that became a Habsburg court, a Napoleonic stage, and finally Milan's most political museum.*

### Origins · 12th c. — 1598

The site has been a building of government since at least the 1100s, when it served as Milan's **Broletto Vecchio** — the meeting hall of the medieval Free Commune. In the 1330s the **Visconti** dukes transformed it into their seat of power, and after them the **Sforza** continued from the same address. [^31]

This was the court of **Ludovico Sforza** ("il Moro"), and indirectly of **Leonardo da Vinci**, who was Ludovico's court engineer between 1482 and 1499. Several of Leonardo's Milanese projects — including *The Last Supper* commission — were negotiated within these walls. [^32]

> **Story · Beatrice's court.** Ludovico's young wife **Beatrice d'Este** (1475–1497) presided over what was probably the most cultured court in Renaissance Italy. Musicians, poets, scholars, and Leonardo himself were her regulars. She died in childbirth at 21; Ludovico never recovered. [^32]

#### Life in Milan, 12th c. — 1598

Court culture in **Visconti and Sforza Milan** was famously theatrical. Banquets at the Palazzo lasted hours, with multiple "services" of food alternating with music, dance, and elaborate sculpted-sugar centrepieces called *trionfi*. Leonardo himself designed mechanical stage devices for court entertainments — turning tables, automated planets — recorded in his notebooks. [^33]

For ordinary Milanese, the Renaissance was felt less as a cultural rebirth than as a sequence of **wars**: French, Spanish, German armies marched in and out of the duchy from 1499 onward. The **sack of Milan in 1500** by Louis XII, the **Battle of Marignano in 1515**, and finally the **Spanish takeover of 1535** ended the city's independence. Daily life shrank around survival: bread prices fixed by the city, **carestia** (famine) every few decades, the *grida* (public proclamations) read aloud in the squares. [^34]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Sugar sculptures** as the centrepiece of banquets — life-sized animals, scenes from mythology, sometimes edible, often gilded.
> - **Court masques** (*intermezzi*) — dance-drama performed between courses of a dinner.
> - **Sumptuary processions** for major court weddings — the bride's dowry literally paraded through the city.
> - **Bread-price laws** posted weekly on cathedral doors and read by criers.

### Transformation · 1598 — 1900

A devastating **fire in 1598** destroyed large parts of the medieval and Renaissance fabric. The palace as we know it today is largely the work of **Giuseppe Piermarini**, the same architect who designed the **Teatro alla Scala** next door. Commissioned by Empress **Maria Theresa of Austria** in 1769, Piermarini reconstructed the palace in calm, monumental **Neoclassical** style between 1773 and 1778. The defining room was the **Sala delle Cariatidi** (Hall of Caryatids). [^31] [^35]

> **Story · Napoleon's stepson lived here.** When Napoleon made Milan the capital of his Kingdom of Italy in 1805, he installed his stepson **Eugène de Beauharnais** in the Palazzo Reale as Viceroy. The young Beauharnais was popular: he hosted balls, opened the palace gardens to citizens. After Napoleon's fall in 1814 he tried to negotiate to keep his throne — and lost. [^12]

Under the Habsburg **restoration** (1814–1859), the palace returned to Austrian use. After Italian unification in 1861, the **House of Savoy** used it occasionally; in **1919** the Crown formally donated it to the Italian state. [^31]

#### Life in Milan, 1598 — 1900

The 17th century was Milan's darkest: **Spanish absolutism**, the catastrophic **plague of 1630** (perhaps the single most-known event in Milanese collective memory, thanks to Manzoni's *I Promessi Sposi*, 1842), and a long economic decline. Life was hemmed in by religious observance — the great cycle of **Catholic feast-days** organised the year more strictly than any civil calendar — and by the omnipresent **Spanish inquisitors** and their Milanese equivalents. [^13] [^36]

The 1700s under **Habsburg enlightened absolutism** were better. Maria Theresa and her son Joseph II reformed schools, abolished some Church privileges, and made Milan a quietly prosperous administrative capital. **Salons** — gatherings in private aristocratic homes for conversation, reading, and music — emerged in the late 1700s as a distinctly female-led cultural institution. The **Salotto Maffei**, hosted by Clara Maffei from 1834, became the most famous in Milan and a meeting point for Risorgimento intellectuals. [^14]

The Risorgimento moments — **1848 Five Days**, **1859 expulsion of Austria** — were street events, but they were *planned* in salons and palaces, including, ironically, in the Habsburg-controlled Palazzo Reale itself, where Milanese nationalists held administrative posts and conducted parallel lives.

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Salon evenings** as the structural unit of cultural and political life. The salon hostess was a real form of power.
> - **Lottery of nobility** — younger sons entered the clergy or military by family decision, often before age 14.
> - **Plague-watcher duty** — citizens took turns watching the city gates for outsiders during outbreaks.
> - **Reading novels aloud** in the evenings to the whole family or social circle — the silent solitary reading of fiction is a 19th-century habit.

### Today · 1919 — present

The palace's defining modern event was the **Allied bombing of 15 August 1943**. Incendiary bombs landed directly on the Sala delle Cariatidi, gutting the room. After the war, restoration architects made a deliberate, contested decision: **leave the damage visible**. [^18] [^37]

> **Story · Guernica in the bomb-scarred hall, 1953.** When Picasso's *Guernica* toured Europe in 1953, the Milanese hung it inside the Sala delle Cariatidi, against the still-blackened walls. The pairing of the painting about war with a room destroyed by war was deliberate. [^37]

Today the palace operates as Milan's premier **major-exhibition venue**, with around 7,000 m² of galleries. [^37]

#### Life in Milan, 1919 — present

This is the century in which Milanese life **secularises**, **industrialises**, and **internationalises**, in waves. The **fascist period** (1922–1943) imposed a fake collective ritual life — torchlit rallies in Piazza del Duomo, "**Sabato fascista**" Saturday afternoon paramilitary exercises, mandatory Saturday-morning school assemblies in Black Shirt uniform. The **Resistance** (1943–1945) and the **Liberation** (25 April 1945) replaced these with their own observances, many still alive — the **Festa della Liberazione** remains a national holiday. [^38]

The **economic miracle** generations changed daily Milanese habits more in twenty years than the previous two centuries had. Television (RAI began broadcasting in 1954), the **Vespa** (mass-produced in Milan from 1946), the rise of the **scooter** and then the small car, the eight-hour day, paid holidays, women entering the workforce, the radical-feminist movement of the 1970s — all of these reshaped what daily life in Milan looked like. [^19]

The palace itself, in this period, has hosted the city's most-attended cultural events — Picasso (1953), Caravaggio (2017), Monet (2011), the Tutankhamun touring shows, and many more, regularly drawing the largest exhibition audiences in Italy. [^37]

> **Lost rituals · what they did then but no longer do**
>
> - **Sunday family lunch** as a near-universal three-hour event for everyone (still common but much eroded).
> - **The corner-bar phone** — most Italians did not have a home phone until well into the 1980s; the local bar's phone was the social telephone.
> - **Hand-written letters** as the default for distant friends.
> - **Live RAI variety shows** as the centre of the weekly TV calendar.

> **New rituals · what we do now that they didn't**
>
> - **Queueing for blockbuster exhibitions** at Palazzo Reale (timed-entry tickets are now standard).
> - **Audio-guides** in eight languages instead of single-language guided tours.
> - **Posting on Instagram** from inside the show — explicitly allowed in most rooms, a clear break from 20th-century museum etiquette.
> - **Buying a museum ticket on a phone** while standing in the piazza.
